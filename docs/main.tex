\begin{abstract}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Any energy-based model whose energy decomposes over local factors has an inference precision matrix that decomposes additively over those factors.
We show that this decomposition, available at random initialisation before any learning, is sufficient to
(i)~score a graph structure by splitting the profiled energy into a \emph{coverage gap} and a \emph{conditioning penalty},
(ii)~extract from the inference residual a directional signal identifying which structural modification would most reduce the score, and
(iii)~build the graph greedily, one factor at a time, by matching pursuit in the space of local correction operators.
Because each candidate factor contributes a localised positive-semidefinite update to the precision matrix, its incremental gain can be evaluated via the Woodbury identity at the cost of a single forward pass.
The algorithm requires only a few steps of inference on random parameters---no training, no labels beyond a small probe batch---and produces a graph structure together with a certificate of what it can and cannot do.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Consider any model in which latent variables are adjusted to minimise a sum of local energies before parameters are updated: predictive coding networks~\citep{rao1999predictive,whittington2017approximation}, deep equilibrium models~\citep{bai2019deep}, Hopfield networks~\citep{ramsauer2021hopfield}, factor graphs in probabilistic graphical models~\citep{koller2009pgm}.
All share a common structure.
A factor graph $\cG$ connects variables $\bv_i \in \R^{d_i}$, partitioned into clamped data $\by$ and free latent states $\bx \in \R^D$, through parameterised transforms $f_1,\dots,f_M$ with parameters $\btheta$.
Local energies $\E_i$ penalise discrepancy between each variable and its incoming predictions, giving total energy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:energy}
    E(\bx;\btheta,\cG,\by) = \textstyle\sum_i \E_i.
\end{equation}
Inference minimises $E$ over $\bx$ with $\btheta$ fixed. Learning adjusts $\btheta$ afterward.
We address a question that precedes both: \emph{which graph $\cG$ should we use?}

Because the energy decomposes over factors, so does its Gauss--Newton Hessian with respect to the free states.
This gives an \textbf{inference precision matrix} $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG = \sum_e \bm{J}_e^\top \bm{\Lambda}_e \bm{J}_e$ that is available at random initialisation, before any parameter learning, and that decomposes additively over edges.
The decomposition is classical~\citep{lauritzen1996graphical,minka2001ep}.
What is new is building a complete structure-learning algorithm on it: a scoring rule that separates into interpretable failure modes (Section~\ref{sec:score}), a directional search signal extracted from the inference residual (Section~\ref{sec:search}), and a greedy construction algorithm with scalable candidate evaluation (Section~\ref{sec:search}).

\section{The Precision Matrix and the Profiled Energy}\label{sec:score}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Construction}\label{sec:construction}

Linearising each transform $f_e$ around the current state $\bx_0$, the Gauss--Newton approximation to the energy Hessian is (Appendix~\ref{app:hessian}):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hessian}
    \nabla^2_{\bx} E \;\approx\; \bm{\Gamma}_\cG \;=\; \sum_e \bm{J}_e^\top \bm{\Lambda}_e\,\bm{J}_e,
\end{equation}
where $\bm{J}_e = \partial f_e / \partial\bx \in \R^{d_{\mathrm{tgt}(e)}\times D}$ is the Jacobian of transform $e$ with respect to the free states, and $\bm{\Lambda}_e \succeq 0$ is the curvature of the local energy at edge $e$.
The approximation is justified when prediction errors are small relative to transform curvature, or when transforms are close to linear---the standard Gauss--Newton regime. Its quality improves as inference reduces residuals. We do not require exact equilibrium; we require that the neglected second-order terms are small relative to the retained first-order terms (Appendix~\ref{app:hessian}).

Define the \textbf{weighted state Jacobian} by stacking curvature-weighted blocks:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:AG}
    \bm{A}_\cG = \begin{bmatrix} \bm{\Lambda}_1^{1/2}\bm{J}_1 \\ \vdots \\ \bm{\Lambda}_M^{1/2}\bm{J}_M \end{bmatrix} \in \R^{m\times D}, \qquad m = \textstyle\sum_e d_{\mathrm{tgt}(e)},
\end{equation}
so that $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG = \bm{A}_\cG^\top \bm{A}_\cG$.
The column space of $\bm{A}_\cG$---the set of constraint-space directions reachable by adjusting free states---is the central object of the analysis.

\subsection{Architecture as preconditioner}\label{sec:preconditioner}

The precision matrix is the operator that gradient-based inference iterates against.
Its eigenspectrum determines which correction directions exist (nonzero eigenvalues), which are resolved quickly (large eigenvalues), and which are resolved slowly or not at all (small or zero eigenvalues).
Choosing a graph structure is therefore \emph{preconditioner design} for the inference problem: topology determines which directions are available, transform families determine their algebraic structure, and the inference budget $T$ defines a spectral gate separating resolved modes from frozen ones.

\subsection{The profiled energy and its decomposition}\label{sec:decomp}

The \textbf{profiled energy} $\phi(\by;\btheta,\cG) = \min_{\bx} E(\bx;\btheta,\cG,\by)$ is the energy remaining after inference.
It is low when the graph can internally reconcile input and target, and high when no setting of the free variables makes all local predictions accurate.

Collecting curvature-weighted prediction errors into a task residual $\bb_{\by} \in \R^m$ (Appendix~\ref{app:residual}), the energy takes the least-squares form $E(\bx_0+\delta\bx) \approx c_{\by} + \frac{1}{2}\|\bm{A}_\cG\delta\bx - \bb_{\by}\|^2$.
Let $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG = \bm{U\Lambda U}^\top$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_D \geq 0$.
Decompose $\bb_{\by}$ into a component within $\mathrm{col}(\bm{A}_\cG)$, with coefficients $c_i$ in the eigenbasis, and an orthogonal component $\bb_\perp$.
After $T$ gradient steps with step size $\eta$ (Appendix~\ref{app:spectral}):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decomp}
    \boxed{\;\phi_T \;=\; \underbrace{\|\bb_\perp\|^2}_{\text{coverage gap}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{D}(1-\eta\lambda_i)^{2T}\,c_i^2}_{\text{conditioning penalty}}.\;}
\end{equation}

This is the central result. The architecture score separates into two independent failure modes.

The \textbf{coverage gap} $\|\bb_\perp\|^2$ is the component of the task residual orthogonal to $\mathrm{col}(\bm{A}_\cG)$.
It satisfies $\bm{A}_\cG^\top\bb_\perp = 0$: no adjustment of any free variable, through any transform, can reduce it.
This is a hard structural limitation---a certificate of what the graph cannot express, regardless of inference budget.
Crucially, $\bb_\perp$ is a \emph{vector}: its direction specifies exactly which new columns $\bm{A}_\cG$ would need to gain.

The \textbf{conditioning penalty} collects reachable directions resolved too slowly.
Eigenvalues $\lambda_i \gg 1/\eta T$ yield factor $(1-\eta\lambda_i)^{2T}\approx 0$: mode resolved.
Eigenvalues $\lambda_i \ll 1/\eta T$ yield factor $\approx 1$: mode frozen, contributing its full residual $c_i^2$ to the score.
The spectral gate at $\lambda_i \sim 1/\eta T$ is an implicit complexity penalty: the graph is credited only for modes it can exploit within its compute budget.

A good architecture minimises both terms.
A deep chain may have full coverage but terrible conditioning (exponentially spread spectrum, most modes below the gate).
A narrow graph may have good conditioning but poor coverage (well-conditioned but missing constraint-space directions).

\subsection{Relation to model evidence}\label{sec:evidence}

The Laplace-approximated log marginal likelihood at the converged state $\bx^*$ is
$-\log p(\by\mid\cG) \approx E(\bx^*;\by) + \frac{1}{2}\log\det(\bm{\Gamma}_\cG) - \frac{D}{2}\log(2\pi)$.
The Occam factor $\frac{1}{2}\log\det(\bm{\Gamma}_\cG) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_i\log\lambda_i$ penalises \emph{large} eigenvalues (over-committed model); the conditioning penalty penalises \emph{small} eigenvalues (unresolvable modes).
These are complementary failure modes controlled by opposite ends of the spectrum.
We use the finite-step profiled energy deliberately: it captures \emph{computational accessibility}---whether the graph can exploit its capacity within $T$ steps---rather than statistical complexity.

\subsection{The energy gap}\label{sec:gap}

A low profiled energy could reflect a flexible column space that accommodates any target.
To measure whether the graph's geometry actually aligns with the task, we compare correct and incorrect labels:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:egap}
    \Delta E = \phi_T(\by_{\mathrm{wrong}}) - \phi_T(\by_{\mathrm{true}}).
\end{equation}
Combining fit and selectivity:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:score}
    \mathrm{Score}(\cG) = -\,\overline{\phi_T(\by_{\mathrm{true}})} + w\cdot\overline{\Delta E},
\end{equation}
averaged over a probe batch ($B\approx 100$) and 3--5 random parameter seeds.

\section{Residual-Driven Structure Search}\label{sec:search}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The decomposition~\eqref{eq:decomp} is not merely a scoring tool.
The direction of the coverage gap is a constructive signal for \emph{how to improve the graph}.

\subsection{Continuous relaxation within a stratum}\label{sec:continuous}

Architecture space is stratified by the rank of $\bm{A}_\cG$.
Within a stratum, the column space rotates smoothly and gradients of $\phi_T$ with respect to continuous architecture parameters are well-defined; between strata, rank changes and the coverage gap can jump.

We introduce edge weights $\balpha \in [0,1]^M$ and operation mixture weights $\bbeta_e \in \R^K$ selecting among $K$ candidate transform types at each edge, so that each edge contributes $\alpha_e\sum_k\beta_e^{(k)}f_e^{(k)}$ to the energy.
The gradient $\partial\phi_T/\partial\alpha_e$ combines two effects: edge $e$ may bring new directions into the column space (reducing coverage gap) or add eigenvalue mass to existing directions (reducing conditioning penalty).
All quantities are computed by backpropagation through unrolled inference; $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG$ and $\bm{A}_\cG$ are never materialised.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Continuous Structure Optimisation}
\label{alg:continuous}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Supergraph $\cG$ with $M$ candidate edges, $K$ transform types per edge
\REQUIRE Data batch $\{(\bx_n,\by_n)\}_{n=1}^B$, inference steps $T$, learning rates $\eta,\eta_\alpha$
\STATE Initialise $\balpha\leftarrow\mathbf{1}/2$, $\bbeta_e\leftarrow\mathbf{1}/K$ for all $e$
\STATE Draw random parameters $\btheta\sim p(\btheta)$; freeze
\FOR{$s=1,\dots,S$}
    \STATE Initialise $\bx^{(0)}$ to small noise; clamp data
    \FOR{$t=1,\dots,T$}
        \STATE $\bx^{(t)}\leftarrow\bx^{(t-1)}-\eta\,\nabla_{\bx} E(\bx^{(t-1)};\btheta,\balpha,\bbeta,\by)$
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Backpropagate through $T,\dots,1$ to obtain $\nabla_{\balpha}\phi_T$, $\nabla_{\bbeta}\phi_T$
    \STATE $\balpha\leftarrow\mathrm{proj}_{[0,1]}(\balpha-\eta_\alpha\nabla_{\balpha}\phi_T)$; normalise $\bbeta_e$ per edge
\ENDFOR
\STATE Discretise: retain edges with $\alpha_e>\tau$; select $\arg\max_k\beta_e^{(k)}$
\RETURN Discrete graph $\cG^*$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Greedy expansion across strata}\label{sec:expansion}

When Algorithm~\ref{alg:continuous} converges and the coverage gap remains large, the graph must expand: the current directions are insufficient, and no reweighting can fix a missing direction.

A candidate expansion $c$ (a new edge, variable, or skip connection) would add a Jacobian block to $\bm{A}_\cG$, contributing new columns.
The first-order energy gain from adding $c$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gain}
    \Delta\phi(c) \;\approx\; -\|\bm{P}_c\,\bb_\perp\|^2,
\end{equation}
where $\bm{P}_c$ projects onto the new directions in the orthogonal complement of the current column space.

This is matching pursuit~\citep{mallat1993matching} in Jacobian space.
The dictionary is the set of local correction operators that candidate transforms contribute to the precision matrix.
The signal is the coverage gap $\bb_\perp$.
Each step selects the dictionary atom best aligned with the unexplained residual.
The greedy structure is natural because graph edits are additive PSD updates to the precision matrix: each candidate's gain can be evaluated against the current graph without recomputing the full model.
In practice, scoring a candidate costs one forward pass of its transform.

Reduction operates symmetrically: edges with $\alpha_e\approx 0$ after continuous relaxation can be pruned.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Residual-Driven Structure Learning}
\label{alg:full}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Initial graph $\cG_0$, data batch, candidate expansions $\cC$, threshold $\varepsilon_r$
\STATE $\cG\leftarrow\cG_0$
\REPEAT
    \STATE Run Algorithm~\ref{alg:continuous} on $\cG$; obtain $\cG^*$ and score $\phi_T^*$
    \STATE Run inference to convergence on $\cG^*$; record residual $\bb_\perp$
    \IF{$\|\bb_\perp\|<\varepsilon_r$}
        \STATE \textbf{terminate}: graph explains the task
    \ENDIF
    \STATE For each $c\in\cC$: estimate $\Delta\phi(c)$ via one forward pass
    \STATE Expand: $\cG\leftarrow\cG^*+\arg\max_c|\Delta\phi(c)|$; draw $\btheta$ for new edge
    \STATE Optionally prune edges with $\alpha_e<\varepsilon_p$
\UNTIL{budget exhausted or residual no longer decreasing}
\RETURN $\cG$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Scalable scoring via Woodbury}\label{sec:woodbury}

The additive structure makes candidate evaluation cheap even exactly.
Maintain $\bm{M}_\cG = \bm{\Gamma}_\cG + \varepsilon\bm{I}$ and its factorisation.
A candidate $c$ contributes a low-rank PSD update $\Delta\bm{\Gamma}_c = \bm{J}_c^\top\bm{\Lambda}_c\bm{J}_c$.
The Woodbury identity gives
\begin{equation}\label{eq:woodbury}
    (\bm{M}_\cG+\bm{J}_c^\top\bm{\Lambda}_c\bm{J}_c)^{-1} = \bm{M}_\cG^{-1} - \bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\bm{J}_c^\top(\bm{\Lambda}_c^{-1}+\bm{J}_c\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\bm{J}_c^\top)^{-1}\bm{J}_c\bm{M}_\cG^{-1},
\end{equation}
reducing per-candidate evaluation from a $D\times D$ inversion to a $d_{\mathrm{tgt}(c)}\times d_{\mathrm{tgt}(c)}$ solve.
When a candidate adds a single correction direction $\bu$, the gain on residual $\bv$ reduces to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rank1}
    \Delta_c = \frac{(\bv^\top\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\bu)^2}{1+\bu^\top\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\bu}\,,
\end{equation}
where the numerator measures alignment with the unresolved residual and the denominator penalises redundancy with existing directions.

\section{How Structure Enters the Precision Matrix}\label{sec:structure}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Each edge contributes a PSD term $\bm{J}_e^\top\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e$ whose algebraic structure reflects the transform, whose weighting reflects the energy, and whose coupling with other terms reflects the topology.
We trace these contributions at random initialisation through the coverage/conditioning decomposition, drawing on standard results in random matrix theory~\citep{marchenko1967distribution,gray2006toeplitz}.

\paragraph{Depth.}
A chain $\bv_0\to\cdots\to\bv_L$ produces effective Jacobians involving products of per-layer matrices whose singular values spread exponentially with depth: $\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}\sim e^{cL}$.
Most eigenvalues of $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG$ fall below the spectral gate---the inference-side analogue of vanishing gradients.

\paragraph{Skip connections.}
A skip edge $\bv_i\to\bv_j$ adds a PSD term with $O(1)$ eigenvalues at random init---no multiplicative composition.
It raises the smallest eigenvalues of $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG$: an additive correction to the preconditioner's spectral spread.

\paragraph{Parallel edges.}
$K$ parallel edges contribute $K$ independent PSD terms whose summed eigenvalues concentrate more tightly around the mean: better conditioning within a fixed budget.

\paragraph{Dense transforms.}
$f(\bv)=\bm{W}\bv$ with i.i.d.\ entries gives a Wishart contribution $\bm{W}^\top\bm{W}$ with isotropic eigenvectors.
No direction is preferred; alignment with task structure is no better than chance.
Dense transforms provide capacity without structural prior.

\paragraph{Convolutional transforms.}
$f(\bv)=\bm{W}*\bv$ gives a block-circulant Jacobian diagonalised by the DFT regardless of filter values.
The precision contribution has eigenvectors fixed to the Fourier basis; only eigenvalues are random.
On natural images, whose residuals have structured frequency content, this separates signal from noise in a way isotropic eigenvectors cannot.

\paragraph{Attention.}
At random init with large key dimension, attention logits concentrate near zero and the Jacobian approximates mean-pooling composed with a random value projection, emphasising position-averaged correction directions.

\paragraph{Recurrence.}
The Jacobian from time $t$ to $T$ involves products $\prod_{s=t}^{T-1}\mathrm{diag}(\sigma'_s)\bm{W}_h$ whose singular values decay with lag; long-range modes are suppressed.
Gated variants introduce additive paths that preserve singular values.

\paragraph{Energy functions.}
Squared error gives $\bm{\Lambda}_e=\bm{I}$ (isotropic).
Cross-entropy gives $\bm{\Lambda}_e=\mathrm{diag}(\bm{p})-\bm{p}\bm{p}^\top$ (PSD on the simplex tangent space, singular in the all-ones direction).

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The precision matrix and its decomposition over cliques are foundational to Gaussian graphical models~\citep{lauritzen1996graphical}; score-based structure learning selects graphs by penalised likelihood or independence tests~\citep{koller2009pgm}.
Our approach uses the same object in a different regime: random initialisation of neural transforms, finite-step inference, and heterogeneous parameterised factors.

The Neural Tangent Kernel~\citep{jacot2018neural} linearises around parameters and produces a Gram matrix governing \emph{learning} dynamics.
TE-NAS~\citep{chen2021tenas} and NASI~\citep{shu2022nasi} use NTK-derived quantities to rank architectures without training, but \citet{mok2022demystifying} show modern architectures leave the NTK regime quickly, and \citet{zhou2024vintk} find NTK metrics ineffective on Vision Transformers because the kernel conflates attention and MLP spectra through end-to-end composition.
The precision-matrix approach avoids this: it decomposes over local factors, linearises ${\sim}20$ inference steps rather than $10^3$--$10^4$ training steps, and discriminates through both coverage and conditioning rather than requiring auxiliary expressivity proxies.

The profiled energy is the data-fit term of the Laplace-approximated marginal likelihood (Section~\ref{sec:evidence}); our score is a training-free surrogate for Bayesian model evidence with a spectral refinement via the conditioning penalty.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:full} is matching pursuit~\citep{mallat1993matching} in Jacobian space; compressed sensing theory~\citep{tropp2004greed} provides convergence guarantees under dictionary conditions (coherence, restricted isometry) whose applicability to neural Jacobian dictionaries is an open question.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

We have developed a structure-learning method for energy-based models resting on the spectral analysis of the inference precision matrix at random initialisation.
The core mechanism is the additive decomposition of the precision matrix over factor-graph edges, which gives four things at once: a decomposed score (coverage and conditioning), a directional search signal (the coverage gap), a greedy construction algorithm (matching pursuit), and scalable candidate evaluation (Woodbury updates).

The framework applies to any model with meaningful iterative inference over internal states.
For feedforward architectures without iterative inference, one can treat activations as free variables with $T=1$, collapsing the conditioning penalty and reducing the score to coverage alone; whether this retains useful discriminative power is an empirical question.

The framework makes concrete predictions.
The profiled energy should correlate with trained accuracy on NAS benchmarks.
The energy gap should track inductive bias: convolutional edges favoured on images, attention on sequences.
Skip connections should reduce the conditioning penalty for deep graphs.
Residual-driven expansion should outperform random search.
The score should remain discriminative on heterogeneous search spaces where existing training-free metrics degrade.

\section{Gauss--Newton Decomposition}\label{app:hessian}

For a single energy term $\E_i(\bv_i,\hat{\bv}_i)$ with prediction $\hat{\bv}_i = f_e(\bv_{\mathrm{src}(e)})$, the second derivative with respect to any free variable block $\bz$ is
\begin{equation}
    \nabla^2_{\bz}\E_i = \bm{J}_e^{(\bz)\top}(\nabla^2_{\hat{v}}\E_i)\bm{J}_e^{(\bz)} + \sum_j(\nabla_{\hat{v}_j}\E_i)\nabla^2_{\bz}[f_e]_j,
\end{equation}
where $\bm{J}_e^{(\bz)}=\partial f_e/\partial\bz$.
The first term is the Gauss--Newton part: $\bm{J}_e^{(\bz)\top}\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e^{(\bz)}$ with $\bm{\Lambda}_e = \nabla^2_{\hat{v}}\E_i\succeq 0$.
The second term involves second derivatives of the transform weighted by the energy gradient, and is neglected when either (a)~prediction errors $\nabla_{\hat{v}}\E_i$ are small, or (b)~transforms are close to linear.
At random initialisation neither condition is guaranteed; the approximation is a modelling choice.
Setting $\bz=\bx$ and summing over edges gives~\eqref{eq:hessian}.

\section{Task Residual}\label{app:residual}

$\bm{A}_\cG\in\R^{m\times D}$ maps state perturbations $\delta\bx$ to changes in curvature-weighted prediction space, with block row $[\bm{A}_\cG]_e = \bm{\Lambda}_e^{1/2}\bm{J}_e$.
The task residual $\bb_{\by}\in\R^m$ stacks curvature-weighted prediction errors $\bm{\Lambda}_e^{1/2}(\hat{\bv}_e - \bv_{\mathrm{tgt}(e)})$ with clamped values imposed.

\section{Spectral Filter}\label{app:spectral}

% Any energy-based model whose energy decomposes over local factors---predictive coding networks, deep equilibrium models, Hopfield networks, factor graphs---has an inference precision matrix that decomposes additively over those factors. We show that this decomposition, available at random initialisation before any parameter learning, is sufficient to (i)~score a graph structure by splitting the profiled energy into a \emph{coverage gap} and a \emph{conditioning penalty}, (ii)~extract from the inference residual a directional signal identifying which structural modification would most reduce the score, and (iii)~build the graph greedily, one factor at a time, by matching pursuit in the space of local correction operators. Because each candidate factor contributes a localised positive-semidefinite update to the precision matrix, its incremental gain can be evaluated against the current graph via the Woodbury identity at the cost of a single forward pass. The resulting algorithm requires only a few steps of inference on randomly initialised parameters---no training, no labels beyond a small probe batch---and produces a graph structure together with a decomposed certificate of what it can and cannot do.

% Any energy-based model whose energy decomposes over local factors has an inference Hessian that decomposes over those same factors. This is a large class: predictive coding networks, deep equilibrium models, Hopfield networks, factor graphs in probabilistic graphical models, and any model where latent variables are adjusted to minimise a sum of local energies before parameters are updated. We consider such models in full generality. Variables are connected by parameterised transforms; some variables are clamped to data, the rest are free. Inference minimises total energy over the free variables with parameters held fixed. Learning adjusts parameters afterward. We address a question that precedes both: \emph{which graph structure should we use?}

% The answer we develop rests on a single object: the Gauss--Newton approximation to the Hessian of the energy with respect to the free states. This is the \textbf{inference precision matrix}. On any factor graph, it decomposes additively over edges, with each edge contributing a positive-semidefinite (PSD) term whose algebraic structure reflects the transform type and whose spectral content reflects that transform's contribution to the inference geometry. The decomposition is a classical property of precision matrices on graphical models~\citep{lauritzen1996graphical,minka2001ep}. What is new is using it, at random initialisation, as the basis for a complete structure-learning algorithm.

% The mathematical argument proceeds in five steps.

% \paragraph{Precision matrix as preconditioner (Section~\ref{sec:precision}).} The precision matrix $\bm{\Gamma}_{\cG} = \sum_e \bm{J}_e^\top\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e$ governs inference dynamics. Its eigenspectrum determines which correction directions are accessible and how quickly they are resolved. The graph topology and transform choices are not merely representational---they design the preconditioner for the inference optimisation problem.

% \paragraph{Coverage and conditioning (Section~\ref{sec:profiled}).} The energy remaining after $T$ inference steps---the \textbf{profiled energy}---separates into two terms: a \emph{coverage gap} (task residual orthogonal to the column space of the weighted state Jacobian, structurally unreachable by any number of inference steps) and a \emph{conditioning penalty} (task residual along directions with small eigenvalues, nominally reachable but practically frozen within the inference budget). This decomposition makes the architecture score interpretable and diagnostic.

% \paragraph{The residual as search signal (Section~\ref{sec:expansion}).} The coverage gap is not merely a scalar measuring shortfall. It is a vector whose direction specifies exactly which new columns the weighted Jacobian would need to gain. A candidate structural modification (a new edge, a skip connection, a new latent variable) would add specific columns. The alignment between those columns and the current coverage gap estimates the candidate's marginal value.

% \paragraph{Matching pursuit (Section~\ref{sec:expansion}).} Because graph edits are additive PSD updates to the precision matrix, candidate selection is a greedy approximation problem. The factor graph defines a dictionary of local correction operators; the task residual defines a signal; each expansion step selects the dictionary atom best aligned with the unexplained residual. This is matching pursuit~\citep{mallat1993matching} in Jacobian space.

% \paragraph{Scalable scoring (Section~\ref{sec:incremental}).} The Woodbury identity provides incremental updates to the regularised inverse of the precision matrix, reducing per-candidate evaluation from a full $D \times D$ inversion to a small $d_{\mathrm{tgt}} \times d_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ solve. This makes the search scalable to large candidate sets.

If $\bm{J}_{e'}$ lies within the existing column space, the addition strengthens existing directions by increasing their eigenvalues, improving conditioning.

% \paragraph{Depth.}
% A chain $\bv_0 \to \bv_1 \to \cdots \to \bv_L$ contributes terms whose effective Jacobians involve products of per-layer matrices. Under random initialisation, singular values of such products spread exponentially with depth: $\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ grows as $e^{cL}$. Most eigenvalues of $\bm{\Gamma}_{\cG}$ become small, and most correction modes fall below the spectral gate. This is the inference-side analogue of vanishing gradients, made explicit through the preconditioner's spectrum.

% \paragraph{Skip connections.}
% A skip edge $\bv_i \to \bv_j$ adds a PSD term with eigenvalues that are $O(1)$ at random init---a single random matrix, no multiplicative composition. When existing paths have collapsed eigenvalues, the skip term raises the smallest eigenvalues of $\bm{\Gamma}_{\cG}$: a direct, additive correction to the preconditioner's spectral spread.

% \paragraph{Parallel edges.}
% $K$ parallel edges of the same type contribute $K$ independent PSD terms. Their sum has eigenvalues concentrating more tightly around the mean than any individual term: better conditioning, more modes accessible within a fixed budget.

% \paragraph{Non-feedforward topologies.}
% Lateral connections, feedback paths, and cycles contribute PSD terms on the same footing. The formalism does not privilege any flow direction.

% \subsection{Transform Families}\label{sec:transforms}

% Each edge's Jacobian $\bm{J}_e$ inherits the algebraic structure of its transform. Because the precision matrix assembles these locally, each transform's structure is visible before compositional mixing.

% \paragraph{Dense transforms.}
% For $f(\bv) = \bm{W}\bv$ with i.i.d.\ entries, the contribution $\bm{W}^\top\bm{W}$ is a Wishart matrix with isotropic eigenvectors (Haar-distributed singular vectors). No direction is preferred; alignment with any particular task structure is no better than chance. Dense transforms provide capacity without structural prior.

% \paragraph{Convolutional transforms.}
% For $f(\bv) = \bm{W} * \bv$, the Jacobian is block-circulant and diagonalised by the discrete Fourier transform regardless of filter values. The precision contribution has eigenvectors fixed to the Fourier basis; only eigenvalues (squared frequency response) are random. The bias is in the \emph{basis}: the precision matrix decomposes the task residual by spatial frequency. On natural images, whose residuals have structured frequency content, this separates signal from noise in a way isotropic eigenvectors cannot.

% \paragraph{Attention transforms.}
% At random init with large key dimension $d$, attention logits $\bm{q}_i^\top\bm{k}_j/\sqrt{d}$ concentrate near zero, softmax approaches uniform weights, and the Jacobian approximates mean-pooling composed with a random value projection. The precision contribution emphasises position-averaged correction directions.

% \paragraph{Recurrent transforms.}
% The Jacobian from time $t$ to time $T$ involves matrix products $\prod_{s=t}^{T-1}\mathrm{diag}(\sigma'_s)\bm{W}_h$ whose singular values decay with lag. Long-range correction directions are suppressed. Gated variants introduce additive paths that can preserve singular values, improving the preconditioner for long-range dependencies.

% \subsection{Energy Functions}\label{sec:energy}

% The curvature $\bm{\Lambda}_e$ weights each edge's contribution. Squared error gives $\bm{\Lambda}_e = \bm{I}$: isotropic weighting. Cross-entropy gives $\bm{\Lambda}_e = \mathrm{diag}(\bm{p}) - \bm{p}\bm{p}^\top$: PSD on the simplex tangent space, adapted to categorical outputs, singular in the all-ones direction. Precision-weighted energies introduce learnable per-dimension scaling.

% \paragraph{Neural architecture search without training.}
% The Neural Tangent Kernel~\citep{jacot2018neural} linearises a model around its parameters and produces a Gram matrix governing learning dynamics. % \paragraph{Sparse approximation.}
% Algorithm~\ref{alg:full} is matching pursuit~\citep{mallat1993matching} in Jacobian space. Whether these conditions hold for dictionaries of neural transform Jacobians is an open question whose resolution could yield non-trivial guarantees on the quality of the greedy architecture relative to the combinatorial optimum.

% \section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
% %═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

% We have developed a structure-learning method for energy-based models that rests on the spectral analysis of the inference precision matrix at random initialisation. The method's core mechanism is the additive decomposition of the precision matrix over factor-graph edges---inherited from the energy's local factorisation and preserved by the Gauss--Newton approximation. This additivity gives four things at once: a decomposed architecture score (coverage and conditioning), a directional search signal (the coverage gap), a greedy construction algorithm (matching pursuit), and scalable candidate evaluation (Woodbury updates).

% The framework applies to models with meaningful iterative inference over internal states: predictive coding networks, deep equilibrium models, energy-based models with iterative refinement, and factor graphs with approximate message passing. For standard feedforward architectures without internal inference, one can treat layer activations as free variables with a single relaxation step ($T = 1$), collapsing the conditioning penalty and reducing the score to coverage alone. Whether this degenerate case retains useful discriminative power is an empirical question.

% \paragraph{Predictions.} The framework makes concrete, falsifiable predictions. The profiled energy should correlate with trained accuracy on NAS benchmarks. The energy gap should track inductive bias across data domains: convolutional edges favoured on images, attention on sequences, both equivalent on unstructured data. Skip connections should visibly reduce the conditioning penalty for deep graphs. Residual-driven expansion should outperform random search. And the score should remain discriminative on heterogeneous search spaces---mixing convolution, attention, and dense transforms---where existing training-free metrics degrade.
