\begin{abstract}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
Any energy-based model whose energy decomposes over local factors has an inference precision matrix that decomposes additively over those factors.
We show that this decomposition is sufficient to score and prune graph structure without retraining: the profiled energy after $T$ inference steps splits into a \emph{coverage gap} (what the graph structurally cannot reach) and a \emph{conditioning penalty} (what it can reach but too slowly), and each edge's marginal contribution to this score can be read from the precision matrix inverse via a single analytic downdate.
The resulting algorithm is the structural analogue of Bayesian model reduction, but scored by computational accessibility rather than variational free energy.
Where model evidence penalises large eigenvalues (over-committed capacity), the profiled energy penalises small eigenvalues (modes that inference cannot resolve within budget).
The method applies to any energy-based model: at random initialisation before any learning, or as post-hoc reduction of a pretrained model.
We focus on the former case, where the algorithm requires only a few steps of inference on random parameters and a small probe batch to produce a graph structure together with a certificate of what it can and cannot do.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Consider any model in which latent variables are adjusted to minimise a sum of local energies before parameters are updated: predictive coding networks~\citep{rao1999predictive,whittington2017approximation}, deep equilibrium models~\citep{bai2019deep}, Hopfield networks~\citep{ramsauer2021hopfield}, factor graphs in probabilistic graphical models~\citep{koller2009pgm}.
All share a common structure.
A factor graph $\cG$ connects variables $\bv_i \in \R^{d_i}$, partitioned into clamped data $\by$ and free latent states $\bx \in \R^D$, through parameterised transforms $f_1,\dots,f_M$ with parameters $\btheta$.
Local energies $\E_i$ penalise discrepancy between each variable and its incoming predictions, giving total energy
\begin{equation}\label{eq:energy}
    E(\bx;\btheta,\cG,\by) = \textstyle\sum_i \E_i.
\end{equation}
Inference minimises $E$ over $\bx$ with $\btheta$ fixed. Learning adjusts $\btheta$ afterward.
We address a question that applies both before and after learning: \emph{which edges does this graph actually need?}

Because the energy decomposes over factors, so does its Gauss--Newton Hessian with respect to the free states.
This gives an \textbf{inference precision matrix} $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG = \sum_e \bm{J}_e^\top \bm{\Lambda}_e \bm{J}_e$ that decomposes additively over edges---a classical property of precision matrices on graphical models~\citep{lauritzen1996graphical,minka2001ep}.
What is new is using this decomposition as the basis for a complete structure-learning algorithm that is the structural analogue of Bayesian model reduction~\citep{friston2018bmr}.

Bayesian model reduction (BMR) fits one full model via variational Bayes, then analytically scores every reduced model that differs only in its priors---\emph{invert once, reduce many}~\citep{friston2016group}.
We apply the same logic to graph structure rather than parameter priors.
Compute the full supergraph's precision matrix inverse once; then for each candidate edge removal, compute the change in the architecture score analytically via a Woodbury downdate.
No refitting, no re-running inference.

The critical departure from BMR is the \emph{score}.
BMR optimises variational free energy, whose Occam factor $\frac{1}{2}\log\det(\bm{\Gamma}_\cG)$ penalises large eigenvalues---a model that is statistically over-committed.
We optimise the \emph{profiled energy} $\phi_T$, whose conditioning penalty penalises small eigenvalues---modes that inference cannot resolve within its compute budget.
These are complementary failure modes controlled by opposite ends of the spectrum.
For architecture selection, where the question is ``can this graph solve the task in $T$ steps?''\ rather than ``does this graph overfit?'', the spectral gate is the relevant penalty.

The method applies at two points in a model's lifecycle.
At \textbf{random initialisation}, before any parameter learning, the precision matrix reflects only topology, transform families, and energy functions---not learned features.
Structure selection at this stage identifies which graph geometry is suited to the task before committing to training.
On a \textbf{pretrained model}, the same precision matrix now reflects learned features, and reduction identifies which edges the trained model is not actually using---post-hoc pruning with an inference-aware score.
We focus on the random-initialisation case throughout, but all results apply identically to pretrained models; the only difference is what the Jacobians contain.

\section{The Precision Matrix and the Profiled Energy}\label{sec:score}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Construction}\label{sec:construction}

Linearising each transform $f_e$ around the current state $\bx_0$, the Gauss--Newton approximation to the energy Hessian is (Appendix~\ref{app:hessian}):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hessian}
    \nabla^2_{\bx} E \;\approx\; \bm{\Gamma}_\cG \;=\; \sum_e \bm{J}_e^\top \bm{\Lambda}_e\,\bm{J}_e,
\end{equation}
where $\bm{J}_e = \partial r_e/\partial\bx \in \R^{d_{\mathrm{tgt}(e)}\times D}$ is the Jacobian of the prediction residual $r_e = f_e(\bv_{\mathrm{src}}) - \bv_{\mathrm{tgt}}$ with respect to the free states, and $\bm{\Lambda}_e \succeq 0$ is the curvature of the local energy at edge $e$.
The approximation is justified when prediction errors are small relative to transform curvature, or when transforms are close to linear---the standard Gauss--Newton regime.
Its quality improves as inference reduces residuals (Appendix~\ref{app:hessian}).

Define the \textbf{weighted state Jacobian} by stacking curvature-weighted blocks:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:AG}
    \bm{A}_\cG = \begin{bmatrix} \bm{\Lambda}_1^{1/2}\bm{J}_1 \\ \vdots \\ \bm{\Lambda}_M^{1/2}\bm{J}_M \end{bmatrix} \in \R^{m\times D}, \qquad m = \textstyle\sum_e d_{\mathrm{tgt}(e)},
\end{equation}
so that $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG = \bm{A}_\cG^\top \bm{A}_\cG$.
The column space of $\bm{A}_\cG$---the set of constraint-space directions reachable by adjusting free states---is the central object of the analysis.

\subsection{Architecture as preconditioner}\label{sec:preconditioner}

The precision matrix is the operator that gradient-based inference iterates against.
Its eigenspectrum determines which correction directions exist (nonzero eigenvalues), which are resolved quickly (large eigenvalues), and which are resolved slowly or not at all (small or zero eigenvalues).
Choosing a graph structure is therefore \emph{preconditioner design} for the inference problem: topology determines which directions are available, transform families determine their algebraic structure, and the inference budget $T$ defines a spectral gate separating resolved modes from frozen ones.

\subsection{The profiled energy and its decomposition}\label{sec:decomp}

The \textbf{profiled energy} $\phi(\by;\btheta,\cG) = \min_{\bx} E(\bx;\btheta,\cG,\by)$ is the energy remaining after inference.
It is low when the graph can internally reconcile input and target, and high when no setting of the free variables makes all local predictions accurate.

Collecting curvature-weighted prediction errors into a task residual $\bb_{\by} \in \R^m$ (Appendix~\ref{app:residual}), the energy takes the least-squares form $E(\bx_0+\delta\bx) \approx c_{\by} + \frac{1}{2}\|\bm{A}_\cG\delta\bx - \bb_{\by}\|^2$.
Let $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG = \bm{U\Lambda U}^\top$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_D \geq 0$.
Decompose $\bb_{\by}$ into a component within $\mathrm{col}(\bm{A}_\cG)$, with coefficients $c_i$ in the eigenbasis, and an orthogonal component $\bb_\perp$.
After $T$ gradient steps with step size $\eta$ (Appendix~\ref{app:spectral}):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:decomp}
    \boxed{\;\phi_T \;=\; \underbrace{\|\bb_\perp\|^2}_{\text{coverage gap}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{D}(1-\eta\lambda_i)^{2T}\,c_i^2}_{\text{conditioning penalty}}.\;}
\end{equation}

This is the central result.
The architecture score separates into two independent failure modes.

The \textbf{coverage gap} $\|\bb_\perp\|^2$ is the component of the task residual orthogonal to $\mathrm{col}(\bm{A}_\cG)$.
It satisfies $\bm{A}_\cG^\top\bb_\perp = 0$: no adjustment of any free variable, through any transform, can reduce it.
This is a hard structural limitation---a certificate of what the graph cannot express, regardless of inference budget.
Crucially, $\bb_\perp$ is a \emph{vector}: its direction specifies exactly which columns $\bm{A}_\cG$ is missing.

The \textbf{conditioning penalty} collects reachable directions resolved too slowly.
Eigenvalues $\lambda_i \gg 1/\eta T$ yield factor $(1-\eta\lambda_i)^{2T}\approx 0$: mode resolved.
Eigenvalues $\lambda_i \ll 1/\eta T$ yield factor $\approx 1$: mode frozen, contributing its full residual $c_i^2$ to the score.
The spectral gate at $\lambda_i \sim 1/\eta T$ is an implicit complexity penalty: the graph is credited only for modes it can exploit within its compute budget.

A good architecture minimises both terms.
A deep chain may have full coverage but terrible conditioning (exponentially spread spectrum, most modes below the gate).
A narrow graph may have good conditioning but poor coverage (well-conditioned but missing constraint-space directions).

\subsection{Relation to model evidence and Bayesian model reduction}\label{sec:evidence}

The Laplace-approximated log marginal likelihood at the converged state $\bx^*$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:laplace}
    -\log p(\by\mid\cG) \;\approx\; \underbrace{E(\bx^*;\by)}_{\phi_\infty} \;+\; \underbrace{\tfrac{1}{2}\log\det(\bm{\Gamma}_\cG)}_{\text{Occam factor}} \;-\; \tfrac{D}{2}\log(2\pi).
\end{equation}
BMR~\citep{friston2018bmr} scores reduced models by the change in variational free energy under modified priors.
For Gaussian posteriors, this reduces to evaluating~\eqref{eq:laplace} at the new prior, which is analytic given the full model's posterior precision and covariance~\citep{friston2016group}.
Our approach has the same \emph{invert-once-reduce-many} structure but replaces the score.

The Occam factor $\frac{1}{2}\sum_i\log\lambda_i$ penalises \emph{large} eigenvalues: a sharply peaked posterior is an over-committed model.
The conditioning penalty $\sum_i(1-\eta\lambda_i)^{2T}c_i^2$ penalises \emph{small} eigenvalues: modes that inference cannot resolve within budget.
These are complementary failure modes controlled by opposite ends of the spectrum.
BMR asks ``does removing this component reduce model evidence?''
We ask ``does removing this edge reduce the graph's ability to resolve the task within its inference budget?''
The answer is read from the same object---the precision matrix inverse---but scored against computational accessibility rather than statistical complexity.

We use the finite-step profiled energy deliberately.
The spectral gate at $\lambda_i \sim 1/\eta T$ captures whether the graph can \emph{actually exploit} its capacity within the inference budget.
The inference budget $T$ is part of the architectural specification, and the score reflects it.

\subsection{The energy gap}\label{sec:gap}

A low profiled energy could reflect a flexible column space that accommodates any target.
To measure whether the graph's geometry actually aligns with the task, we compare correct and incorrect labels:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:egap}
    \Delta E = \phi_T(\by_{\mathrm{wrong}}) - \phi_T(\by_{\mathrm{true}}).
\end{equation}
Combining fit and selectivity:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:score}
    \mathrm{Score}(\cG) = -\,\overline{\phi_T(\by_{\mathrm{true}})} + w\cdot\overline{\Delta E},
\end{equation}
averaged over a probe batch ($B\approx 100$) and 3--5 random parameter seeds.
The first term measures how well the graph reconciles the data; the second measures whether this reconciliation is selective.

\section{Structure Search via Leverage-Score Reduction}\label{sec:search}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The decomposition~\eqref{eq:decomp} is not merely a scoring tool.
Because each edge contributes an independent PSD term to the precision matrix, the marginal contribution of every edge can be evaluated analytically from a single matrix inverse.
This yields a one-algorithm approach to structure learning: start from the full candidate graph, score all edges simultaneously, and prune.

\subsection{The supergraph and its inverse}\label{sec:supergraph}

Let $\cG_{\mathrm{full}}$ be the supergraph containing all $M$ candidate edges.
Run $T$ steps of inference on $\cG_{\mathrm{full}}$ with random (or pretrained) parameters $\btheta$ to obtain the state $\bx^{(T)}$, the task residual $\bb_{\by}$, and the Jacobians $\bm{J}_e$ at each edge.
Form the regularised precision matrix and invert once:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:MG}
    \bm{M}_\cG = \bm{\Gamma}_\cG + \varepsilon\bm{I} = \sum_e \bm{J}_e^\top\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e + \varepsilon\bm{I}, \qquad \bm{M}_\cG^{-1} \;\text{computed once}.
\end{equation}
This is the analogue of fitting the full model in BMR.
The ridge parameter $\varepsilon$ provides numerical stability at rank boundaries and softens the spectral gate from~\eqref{eq:decomp} into a continuous filter.
Cost: $O(D^3)$ for the inversion, plus $O(D^2 m)$ for the Jacobian products.
For the model scales we consider ($D \lesssim 10{,}000$), this is seconds on a GPU.

\subsection{Per-edge leverage scores}\label{sec:leverage}

The \textbf{leverage score} of edge $e$ measures how much of the graph's inference capacity depends uniquely on that edge:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:leverage}
    \ell_e = \mathrm{tr}\!\left(\bm{\Lambda}_e\,\bm{J}_e\,\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\,\bm{J}_e^\top\right).
\end{equation}
High leverage means edge $e$ contributes directions to the column space of $\bm{A}_\cG$ that no other edge provides---removing it would open a coverage gap or degrade conditioning.
Low leverage means the edge is redundant: its directions are already well-represented by the remaining edges.

The leverage score falls directly out of $\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}$.
All $M$ evaluations are independent and parallelisable, each costing $O(D^2\,d_{\mathrm{tgt}(e)})$---a single matrix multiply per edge.

The connection to BMR is direct.
In BMR, a parameter is removed by shrinking its prior variance to zero; the change in free energy is analytic given the posterior precision~\citep{friston2018bmr}.
Here, an edge is removed by subtracting its PSD term $\bm{J}_e^\top\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e$ from $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG$; the change in $\phi_T$ is analytic given $\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}$, via the Woodbury downdate:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:downdate}
    (\bm{M}_\cG - \bm{J}_e^\top\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e)^{-1} = \bm{M}_\cG^{-1} + \bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\bm{J}_e^\top(\bm{\Lambda}_e^{-1} - \bm{J}_e\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\bm{J}_e^\top)^{-1}\bm{J}_e\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}.
\end{equation}
The expensive inversion is over the small $d_{\mathrm{tgt}(e)} \times d_{\mathrm{tgt}(e)}$ matrix, not the full state space.
When an edge contributes a single correction direction $\bu$, the change in score on a residual $\bv$ reduces to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:rank1}
    \Delta_e = \frac{(\bv^\top\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\bu)^2}{1 - \bu^\top\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\bu}\,,
\end{equation}
where the numerator measures how much of the resolved residual depends on this edge and the denominator captures the degree to which other edges can compensate.

\subsection{Pruning algorithm}\label{sec:pruning}

The leverage scores define a natural pruning procedure.
In the simplest case, threshold: remove all edges with $\ell_e < \tau$ and evaluate $\phi_T$ on the reduced graph.
Because leverage scores are computed from the full interaction structure, they account for redundancy between edges---an edge may have high standalone value but low leverage because its contribution is duplicated by its neighbours.

For greater precision, prune iteratively: remove the lowest-leverage fraction of edges, update $\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}$ via the Woodbury downdate, recompute leverage scores, and repeat.
Iterative pruning catches joint redundancies that one-shot scoring misses---two edges that are individually essential but jointly redundant.
Algorithm~\ref{alg:reduce} gives the procedure.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Inference-Aware Structure Reduction}
\label{alg:reduce}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\REQUIRE Supergraph $\cG_{\mathrm{full}}$ with $M$ candidate edges, $K$ transform types per edge
\REQUIRE Data batch $\{(\bx_n,\by_n)\}_{n=1}^B$, inference steps $T$, prune fraction $\rho$, score threshold $\delta$
\STATE Draw random parameters $\btheta\sim p(\btheta)$ (or load pretrained $\btheta$)
\STATE Run $T$ inference steps on $\cG_{\mathrm{full}}$; record state $\bx^{(T)}$, residual $\bb_{\by}$
\STATE Compute Jacobians $\bm{J}_e$ and curvatures $\bm{\Lambda}_e$ for all edges $e$
\STATE Form $\bm{M}_\cG = \sum_e \bm{J}_e^\top\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e + \varepsilon\bm{I}$; compute $\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}$
\STATE Evaluate $\phi_T^{\mathrm{full}}$ on $\cG_{\mathrm{full}}$
\REPEAT
    \STATE Compute leverage $\ell_e = \mathrm{tr}(\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}\bm{J}_e^\top)$ for all edges $e$
    \STATE Identify lowest-leverage fraction $\rho$ of active edges
    \FOR{each edge $e$ in removal set}
        \STATE Update $\bm{M}_\cG^{-1}$ via Woodbury downdate~\eqref{eq:downdate}
        \STATE Remove $e$ from active edge set
    \ENDFOR
    \STATE Evaluate $\phi_T$ on reduced graph
\UNTIL{$\phi_T - \phi_T^{\mathrm{full}} > \delta$ \textbf{or} no edges remain below threshold}
\STATE Restore last removal batch (score exceeded tolerance)
\RETURN Reduced graph $\cG^*$, per-edge leverage scores, decomposed $\phi_T$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\paragraph{Output.}
The algorithm returns three things: the reduced graph $\cG^*$, the leverage score of every retained edge (a continuous measure of structural importance), and the decomposed profiled energy $\phi_T = \|\bb_\perp\|^2 + \sum_i(1-\eta\lambda_i)^{2T}c_i^2$, which is a certificate of what the reduced graph can and cannot do.
The coverage gap identifies structurally unreachable error directions; the conditioning penalty identifies directions that are nominally reachable but practically frozen.

\paragraph{Computational cost.}
The dominant cost is the initial $O(D^3)$ inversion.
Each pruning round costs $O(|\text{batch}| \cdot D^2 \cdot d_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ for the Woodbury updates plus $O(M \cdot D^2 \cdot d_{\mathrm{tgt}})$ for leverage recomputation.
For $D \lesssim 10{,}000$ and $M \lesssim 1{,}000$, the entire procedure completes in seconds.
For larger $D$, the explicit inverse can be replaced by iterative solves against $\bm{M}_\cG$ (conjugate gradients), with leverage scores estimated via stochastic trace estimators~\citep{hutchinson1989stochastic}.

\subsection{Connection to matching pursuit}\label{sec:pursuit}

The \emph{forward} direction of structure search---adding edges one at a time to a minimal graph---is matching pursuit~\citep{mallat1993matching} in Jacobian space.
The dictionary is the set of local correction operators that candidate transforms contribute to the precision matrix; the signal is the coverage gap $\bb_\perp$; each step selects the dictionary atom best aligned with the unexplained residual.
The energy gain from adding candidate $c$ is approximately $\Delta\phi(c) \approx -\|\bm{P}_c\bb_\perp\|^2$, where $\bm{P}_c$ projects onto the new directions in the orthogonal complement of the current column space.

The backward direction---pruning from the supergraph, as in Algorithm~\ref{alg:reduce}---is the same problem viewed from the other end.
Both exploit the additive PSD structure of the precision matrix.
The backward direction is preferred because it starts with full information about the interaction structure: every edge's contribution is visible from step one, and leverage scores account for redundancy automatically.
The forward direction suffers from an exploration problem---candidates touching disconnected variables have zero projection into the active eigenbasis and cannot be discovered---which the backward direction avoids entirely.

\section{How Structure Enters the Precision Matrix}\label{sec:structure}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

Each edge contributes a PSD term $\bm{J}_e^\top\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e$ whose algebraic structure reflects the transform, whose weighting reflects the energy, and whose coupling with other terms reflects the topology.
We trace these contributions at random initialisation through the coverage/conditioning decomposition, drawing on standard results in random matrix theory~\citep{marchenko1967distribution,gray2006toeplitz}.

\paragraph{Depth.}
A chain $\bv_0\to\cdots\to\bv_L$ produces effective Jacobians involving products of per-layer matrices whose singular values spread exponentially with depth: $\sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}\sim e^{cL}$.
Most eigenvalues of $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG$ fall below the spectral gate---the inference-side analogue of vanishing gradients.

\paragraph{Skip connections.}
A skip edge $\bv_i\to\bv_j$ adds a PSD term with $O(1)$ eigenvalues at random init---no multiplicative composition.
It raises the smallest eigenvalues of $\bm{\Gamma}_\cG$: an additive correction to the preconditioner's spectral spread.
In the leverage-score framework, skip connections in deep graphs will have high leverage precisely because they provide directions that the chain alone cannot resolve within budget.

\paragraph{Parallel edges.}
$K$ parallel edges contribute $K$ independent PSD terms whose summed eigenvalues concentrate more tightly around the mean: better conditioning within a fixed budget.
The leverage of each individual parallel edge will be low (redundant with its neighbours), and the pruning algorithm will naturally thin them to the minimum set needed for adequate conditioning.

\paragraph{Dense transforms.}
$f(\bv)=\bm{W}\bv$ with i.i.d.\ entries gives a Wishart contribution $\bm{W}^\top\bm{W}$ with isotropic eigenvectors.
No direction is preferred; alignment with task structure is no better than chance.
Dense transforms provide capacity without structural prior.

\paragraph{Convolutional transforms.}
$f(\bv)=\bm{W}*\bv$ gives a block-circulant Jacobian diagonalised by the DFT regardless of filter values.
The precision contribution has eigenvectors fixed to the Fourier basis; only eigenvalues are random.
On natural images, whose residuals have structured frequency content, this separates signal from noise in a way isotropic eigenvectors cannot.

\paragraph{Attention.}
At random init with large key dimension, attention logits concentrate near zero and the Jacobian approximates mean-pooling composed with a random value projection, emphasising position-averaged correction directions.

\paragraph{Recurrence.}
The Jacobian from time $t$ to $T$ involves products $\prod_{s=t}^{T-1}\mathrm{diag}(\sigma'_s)\bm{W}_h$ whose singular values decay with lag; long-range modes are suppressed.
Gated variants introduce additive paths that preserve singular values.

\paragraph{Energy functions.}
Squared error gives $\bm{\Lambda}_e=\bm{I}$ (isotropic).
Cross-entropy gives $\bm{\Lambda}_e=\mathrm{diag}(\bm{p})-\bm{p}\bm{p}^\top$ (PSD on the simplex tangent space, singular in the all-ones direction).

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\paragraph{Bayesian model reduction.}
BMR~\citep{friston2018bmr,friston2016group} fits a full model via variational Bayes, then analytically scores every reduced model that differs only in its priors.
For Gaussian posteriors under the Laplace approximation, the reduced free energy has a closed form involving the posterior precision and covariance.
Our approach inherits the same \emph{invert-once-reduce-many} structure but operates on graph structure rather than parameter priors, and scores by $\phi_T$ rather than variational free energy.
The key difference is which end of the spectrum is penalised: BMR's Occam factor penalises large eigenvalues (statistical over-commitment), while the conditioning penalty penalises small eigenvalues (computational inaccessibility).
For structure selection---where the question is whether the graph can solve the task within its inference budget---the latter is the relevant criterion.
BMR has recently been applied to neural network pruning~\citep{beckers2024bmrs}, where parameters are removed by shrinking their prior variance to zero.
Our method removes \emph{edges} by subtracting their PSD contributions from the precision matrix, which is the natural unit of structure for energy-based models whose energy decomposes over factors.

\paragraph{Structure learning in graphical models.}
The precision matrix and its decomposition over cliques are foundational to Gaussian graphical models~\citep{lauritzen1996graphical}; score-based structure learning selects graphs by penalised likelihood or independence tests~\citep{koller2009pgm}.
Our approach uses the same object in a different regime: random initialisation of neural transforms, finite-step inference, and heterogeneous parameterised factors rather than fitted tabular conditionals.

\paragraph{Neural architecture search without training.}
The Neural Tangent Kernel~\citep{jacot2018neural} linearises around parameters and produces a Gram matrix governing \emph{learning} dynamics.
TE-NAS~\citep{chen2021tenas} and NASI~\citep{shu2022nasi} use NTK-derived quantities to rank architectures without training, but \citet{mok2022demystifying} show modern architectures leave the NTK regime quickly, and \citet{zhou2024vintk} find NTK metrics ineffective on Vision Transformers because the kernel conflates attention and MLP spectra through end-to-end Jacobian composition.
The precision-matrix approach avoids this: it decomposes over local factors rather than composed parameter blocks, linearises ${\sim}20$ inference steps rather than $10^3$--$10^4$ training steps, and discriminates through both coverage and conditioning rather than requiring auxiliary expressivity proxies.

\paragraph{Sparse approximation.}
The forward direction of structure search (Section~\ref{sec:pursuit}) is matching pursuit~\citep{mallat1993matching} in Jacobian space.
Compressed sensing theory~\citep{tropp2004greed} provides convergence guarantees under dictionary conditions (coherence, restricted isometry) whose applicability to neural Jacobian dictionaries is an open question.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

We have developed a structure-learning method for energy-based models that applies the logic of Bayesian model reduction to graph structure, scored by the profiled energy rather than variational free energy.
The core mechanism is the additive decomposition of the inference precision matrix over factor-graph edges, which gives three things at once: a decomposed architecture score (coverage gap and conditioning penalty), per-edge leverage scores from a single matrix inverse, and an efficient pruning algorithm via Woodbury downdates.

The framework applies to any model with an energy that decomposes over local factors.
At random initialisation, it selects graph structure before any training---topology, transform families, and connectivity pattern---based on the precision geometry alone.
On a pretrained model, the same algorithm performs post-hoc structural pruning with an inference-aware score, identifying edges whose learned contributions are redundant or below the spectral gate.

The framework makes concrete predictions.
The profiled energy should correlate with trained accuracy on NAS benchmarks.
The energy gap should track inductive bias: convolutional edges favoured on images, attention on sequences.
Skip connections should have high leverage in deep graphs.
Leverage-based pruning should outperform random pruning and match or exceed BMR-based pruning on energy-based models, because the score reflects computational accessibility rather than statistical complexity.
The method should remain discriminative on heterogeneous search spaces---mixing convolution, attention, and dense transforms---where existing training-free metrics degrade.

\section{Gauss--Newton Decomposition}\label{app:hessian}

For a single energy term $\E_i(\bv_i,\hat{\bv}_i)$ with prediction $\hat{\bv}_i = f_e(\bv_{\mathrm{src}(e)})$, the second derivative with respect to any free variable block $\bz$ is
\begin{equation}
    \nabla^2_{\bz}\E_i = \bm{J}_e^{(\bz)\top}(\nabla^2_{\hat{v}}\E_i)\bm{J}_e^{(\bz)} + \sum_j(\nabla_{\hat{v}_j}\E_i)\nabla^2_{\bz}[f_e]_j,
\end{equation}
where $\bm{J}_e^{(\bz)}=\partial f_e/\partial\bz$.
The first term is the Gauss--Newton part: $\bm{J}_e^{(\bz)\top}\bm{\Lambda}_e\bm{J}_e^{(\bz)}$ with $\bm{\Lambda}_e = \nabla^2_{\hat{v}}\E_i\succeq 0$.
The second term involves second derivatives of the transform weighted by the energy gradient, and is neglected when either (a)~prediction errors $\nabla_{\hat{v}}\E_i$ are small, or (b)~transforms are close to linear.
At random initialisation neither condition is guaranteed; the approximation is a modelling choice whose quality improves as inference reduces residuals.
Setting $\bz=\bx$ and summing over edges gives~\eqref{eq:hessian}.

\section{Task Residual}\label{app:residual}

$\bm{A}_\cG\in\R^{m\times D}$ maps state perturbations $\delta\bx$ to changes in curvature-weighted prediction space, with block row $[\bm{A}_\cG]_e = \bm{\Lambda}_e^{1/2}\bm{J}_e$.
The task residual $\bb_{\by}\in\R^m$ stacks curvature-weighted prediction errors $\bm{\Lambda}_e^{1/2}(f_e(\bv_{\mathrm{src}}) - \bv_{\mathrm{tgt}(e)})$ with clamped values imposed.

\section{Spectral Filter}\label{app:spectral}

On the quadratic model $E = c + \frac{1}{2}\|\bm{A}\delta\bx-\bb\|^2$ with $\bm{\Gamma}=\bm{A}^\top\bm{A}=\bm{U\Lambda U}^\top$, gradient descent from $\delta\bx^{(0)}=0$ gives $\delta\bx^{(T)}=\bm{U}[\bm{I}-(\bm{I}-\eta\bm{\Lambda})^T]\bm{\Lambda}^{-1}\bm{U}^\top\bm{A}^\top\bb$.
Decomposing $\bb = \bm{A}\bm{U}\bc + \bb_\perp$ with $\bc\in\R^D$ and $\bb_\perp\perp\mathrm{col}(\bm{A})$:
\begin{equation}
    \phi_T = \|\bb_\perp\|^2 + \sum_{i=1}^D(1-\eta\lambda_i)^{2T}c_i^2.
\end{equation}
Large $\lambda_i$: factor $\approx 0$, mode resolved. Small $\lambda_i$: factor $\approx 1$, full residual. Gate at $\lambda_i \sim 1/\eta T$.
